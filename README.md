## Overview

**Unfit for the Archives** is an interactive digital platform that reimagines what an archive can be. It investigates how institutional systems, prisons, internment camps, empires, books, technological infrastructures, and archives themselves shape what enters the historical record, and what gets left out.

Drawing on theorists including **Michel Foucault**, **Jacques Derrida**, **Giorgio Agamben**, and **François Hartog**, the project asks: *who gets to be remembered, and on whose terms?*

At its core, UFTA centers voices and experiences that institutional power has historically rendered invisible. Memory is shaped not only by what survives, but by what systems choose, or choose not to preserve.

> ⚠️ **Content Warning:** This site contains material related to institutional violence, detention, colonialism, systemic inequality, and other sensitive topics. Please proceed with care.

## Sections

Each section of the site examines a different institutional site of power and its relationship to memory, documentation, and erasure.

| Section | Theme |
|---|---|
| 🏛️ **Prisons** | How carceral systems control visibility, identity, and narrative |
| ⛺ **Camps** | Bureaucracy, displacement, and the suspension of rights |
| 📖 **Books** | What is written, published, taught, and what is deliberately omitted |
| 🌍 **Empires** | How colonial power archives itself as inevitable and erases what it subsumes |
| 💻 **Technological Systems** | Algorithms, data regimes, and the politics of digital legibility |
| 🗄️ **Archives** | Archives as active constructions of authority, not neutral storage |

## Features

### Interactive Mini-Games
Each section includes a purpose-built learning game designed to make difficult material more approachable and engaging:

- **Prison → Case Files** — Review "case files" to understand how carceral systems classify and reduce individuals
- **Camps → Day In The Life** — Navigate the bureaucratic logic of displacement and registration
- **Books → The Censor's Desk** — Explore censorship, banned texts, and editorial erasure
- **Empires → The Map Room** — Trace how colonial power constructs and legitimizes its own history
- **Technological Systems → The Audit** — Examine how data systems encode assumptions about who is legible
- **Archives → In The Dark** — Question what gets preserved and why

Flippable flashcard-style modals in every section, surfacing key concepts and contextual facts for each topic.

### Flip Fact Cards
At-a-glance statistics displayed as hover-flip cards (e.g., overcrowding rates, recidivism data, cost of incarceration), anchoring abstract themes into reality.

### Primary Source Excerpts
Clickable thumbnails of primary documents, like letters, records, archival images, open in modals with optional transcriptions. These are the actual voices the formal archive has marginalized.

### Embedded Source PDFs
Scholarly texts and primary documents are embedded directly in each section (Derrida, Caswell, SlaveVoyages records, and more), making the foundation of the project transparent and accessible.

### Responsive Navigation & Animated Intro
- Smooth word-by-word animated text on page load
- Hamburger menu for mobile
- Persistent top nav linking all six sections
- Section-level carousel overview on the homepage

## Theoretical Framework

The project is grounded in critical archival theory and critical theory more broadly:

- **Jacques Derrida** — *Archive Fever*: archives are not passive collections but institutions that construct authority over the past
- **Michel Foucault** — Institutions as systems of discipline that organize bodies, behavior, and knowledge
- **Giorgio Agamben** — "Bare life" and exclusion from political recognition
- **François Hartog** — "Regimes of historicity" and how societies reinterpret the past through the present
- **Michelle Caswell**, **Jarrett Drake**, and others in critical/reparative archival studies
