Overview

Unfit for the Archives is an interactive digital platform that reimagines what an archive can be.

This project explores how institutional systems such as prisons, camps, empires, books, technological systems, and archives themselves shape what becomes part of the historical record. Michel Foucault describes modern institutions as systems of discipline that organize bodies, behavior, and knowledge, producing structures that control both people and the narratives surrounding them.

At its core, Unfit for the Archives focuses on voices and experiences that institutional systems have historically rendered invisible.

By bringing these perspectives together, the project asks how memory is shaped not only by what survives, but by what institutions choose to preserve. As François Hartog argues, societies constantly reinterpret the past through the lens of the present, creating shifting “regimes of historicity” that influence how history is written, remembered, and forgotten.

Prison

Prisons are often framed as sites of justice or rehabilitation. This section looks instead at how incarceration functions as a system of control over visibility, identity, and a person's story.

Within carceral structures, records are produced, bodies are classified, and lives are reduced to files. Some stories are documented in detail, and others disappear entirely. Here, the prison is understood not only as a physical space, but as an archive in motion, one that determines whose pain is recorded, whose growth is acknowledged, and whose humanity is obscured.

This section asks what it means to remember beyond punishment, and what forms of care might exist outside institutional containment.

Camps

Camps emerge in moments of crisis, war, displacement, and emergency, often described as temporary or necessary.

Yet they reveal how quickly rights can be stripped away and how easily lives can be reorganized under an administrative rule.

In camps, people are registered, counted, and managed. Bureaucracy replaces biography. Survival replaces stability. The camp becomes a space where memory is compressed and futures are held in suspension.

This section reflects on impermanence as a form of power, and on the fragile line between protection and confinement.

Books

Books carry authority. They preserve knowledge, legitimize ideas, and circulate stories across generations.

But books also exclude. Decisions about what is written, published, translated, and taught shape cultural memory just as forcefully as any prison wall.

Silence can be editorial. Absence can be institutional. What's left unsaid and what is missing are intentional, powerful actions used to shape narratives and maintain control within organizations.

This section considers the book as both vessel and filter, asking who gets to author history, and whose stories remain unwritten, out of print, or deliberately forgotten.

Empires

Empires don't only conquer land, they organize time, language, and legitimacy. They leave histories labeled official that frame expansion as destiny and domination as progress.

Through maps, decrees, and monuments, empires archive themselves as inevitable.

Giorgio Agamben’s concept of “bare life” highlights how certain individuals are excluded from full political recognition, existing within systems of authority while remaining outside the protections those systems claim to provide.

At the same time, they displace, rename, and erase the worlds they subsume.

This section traces how power sustains itself through narrative continuity, and how resistance persists in fragments, counter-memories, and reclaimed names.

Technological Systems

Technology is often described as a neutral infrastructure with different tools, platforms, databases, and algorithms. Yet every system encodes assumptions about efficiency, value, risk, and worth.

Data is sorted. Profiles are generated. Behaviors are predicted. What can be measured becomes what can be governed.

This section explores how technological systems quietly structure visibility and invisibility, shaping not only what is stored, but what is knowable, and who is legible within digital life.

Archives

Archives are often understood as neutral places where history is stored and preserved. Yet scholars have long argued that archives are shaped by systems of power that determine what is recorded and what is excluded. As Jacques Derrida suggests in Archive Fever, archives are not passive collections of memory but institutions that actively construct authority over the past.

To archive is to decide what matters. To preserve is also to exclude.

Institutional archives often reinforce authority while marginalizing voices deemed disruptive, incomplete, unverified, or unfit.
